#### Welcome to The Fresh Connection

Welcome to The Fresh Connection, a struggling fruit juice manufacturer facing critical supply chain challenges. Whether played individually or in a team, participants must make strategic decisions to optimize the supply chain, improve performance, and turn the business around. Ready to take on the challenge?

#### Roles and responsibilities

Participants take on leadership roles within the management team of The Fresh Connection. While the game is typically played in groups of four, it can also be played with fewer players or individually. Each participant is assigned a specific role:

- **Vice President Purchasing**
- **Vice President Operations**
- **Vice President Sales**
- **Vice President Supply Chain**

Regardless of the number of players, success depends on making well-informed, cross-functional decisions. Strategic alignment remains key, whether collaborating with teammates or managing multiple roles independently.

#### VP Purchasing

**The VP of Purchasing** is responsible for sourcing components under the best possible terms from The Fresh Connection’s suppliers. This role involves supplier selection, evaluating their terms and characteristics, and at times making strategic decisions on contract renewals or terminations. The VP of Purchasing plays a critical role in aligning supply strategy with the business strategy and ensures cost-effective procurement, maintaining supplier reliability, and optimizing inbound supply performance.

#### VP Operations

**The VP of Operations** is responsible for managing capacity of production facilities and warehouses. This role oversees work shifts, appropriate staff training, and determines the allocation of space and manpower for inbound and outbound warehouses and tank yard. By maintaining flexibility in production, controlling costs, and ensuring high production schedule adherence, the VP of Operations plays a crucial role in balancing capacity, efficiency and product availability. Strategic decision-making in this area directly impacts overall operational performance and cost management.

#### VP Sales

**The VP of Sales** oversees product sales and customer agreements. This role negotiates key terms such as service levels, shelf life, and promotional pressure while considering customer needs and ensuring favorable conditions for The Fresh Connection. A well-executed sales strategy can lead to higher sales prices, provided that the company delivers on its commitments. Over-promising results in penalties which erodes margins. As sales drive revenue, the VP of Sales plays a critical role in ensuring profitability and market success.

#### VP Supply Chain Management

**The VP of Supply Chain** is the glue that connects all roles, ensuring smooth collaboration between purchasing, operations, and sales. This role aligns the supply chain strategy with the business strategy and optimizes inventory planning and policies to keep operations running efficiently. By strategically deploying safety stock, the VP of Supply Chain mitigates risks from unreliable suppliers, production constraints, and demand volatility ensuring that customer commitments are met and overall performance remains stable.

#### Strategy

Each Vice President makes decisions independently, but a well-aligned strategy is essential for achieving the best results.

For example, if the VP of Sales commits to high service levels with customers while the VP of Supply Chain reduces stock levels, this misalignment could lead to stockouts and unfulfilled orders.

Similarly, if the VP of Purchasing negotiates low-cost materials but the VP of Operations cannot maintain production quality as a result, overall performance will suffer.

To avoid conflicting decisions, it is crucial to discuss strategies across all roles, ensuring that all functions are aligned toward the company's success.

#### Approach

The simulation reflects the complexity of a real-world company, featuring a diverse range of products, suppliers, and customers. Rather than attempting to gather every detail before making a decision, participants should focus on key strategic areas.

Each Vice President should prioritize the most relevant data for their role. A good starting point is the My Company and functional Role dashboards and the Finance page, which provide targeted insights for effective decision-making.

The Inbox provides additional important scenario information in each round. By focusing on the most critical factors, participants can drive measurable improvements in The Fresh Connection’s performance.

#### The Fresh Connection

The Fresh Connection is a fruit juice manufacturer based in the Netherlands. While known for its high-quality products, the company has recently faced supply chain challenges due to ad hoc management and a lack of clear strategy.

However, under the leadership of CEO Bob McLaren and the new management team, there is optimism about the company’s future and the potential for operational improvements.

#### Customers and products

The Fresh Connection produces a range of juice flavors in several packaging sizes. More details about the product range, components and trade units can be found in the "Information" section. Finished products are delivered to The Fresh Connection’s customers, which include retail companies such as supermarkets. Deliveries take place the day after a customer places an order.

#### Product storage and shelf life

The Fresh Connection products are stored on pallets in the finished goods warehouse. They stay there until a delivery is made, or until their shelf life has expired.

The finished goods have, from the time of production, a limited shelf life. The customers claim a significant part of this shelf life, usually 60% to 80%. This leaves The Fresh Connection with an "internal" shelf life of 20% to 40%. In case the shelf life is expired, the product will unfortunately have to be destroyed.

The Fresh Connection does not have its own fleet to deliver to its customer’s distribution centres and instead outsources the transportation to an extremely reliable partner.

#### The production process

The production process consists of two key steps: mixing and bottling. The juice is bottled immediately after mixing, with no intermediate storage required. All packaging sizes are initially bottled on the same bottling line. Once bottled, the products are stored in The Fresh Connection’s outbound warehouse.

#### The components

A finished product consists of fruit pulp mix, additives, and packaging. The juice formula, made from fruit pulp mix and additives like Vitamin C, gives The Fresh Connection’s products their distinct flavor. This formula has been one of the company’s closely guarded secrets for decades. More details can be found in the Bill of Materials (BoM) within the "Information" section.

#### The suppliers

The Fresh Connection operates with a global supplier network, with each component sourced from a different supplier. Packaging materials are typically sourced from local or regional suppliers, while fruit pulp is purchased from fruit traders and producers worldwide.

Each supplier has different available capacity and lead times which depend on geographic location. Several key terms like quality, reliability, delivery window, payment terms, trade units, or transport modes can be negotiated which result in different component pricing from the supplier.

These and other available information require careful evaluation when selecting suppliers and negotiating supply contracts.

#### Rounds

The Fresh Connection business game is played in rounds and each round represents six months of decision-making for the company. A round consists of a series of decisions that determine company performance. Once the round is completed by saving your decisions and then calculated, the game progresses to the next round. There is no carryover effect from one round to the next—decisions made in previous rounds do not have a long-term impact on future rounds.

#### Scores

The main objective of the business game is to achieve the highest possible **Return on Investment (ROI)**, making The Fresh Connection as profitable as possible. To accomplish this, participants must focus on aligning decisions with a chosen business strategy to:

- **Maximizing revenue**
- **Optimizing costs**
- **Managing investments**, such as inventory, payment terms, and machinery

The overall team performance is ranked based on the ROI score. In addition to the team ROI, each role receives an individual score based on performance within that role. However, individual scores do not affect the team's ranking.

#### Decisions

In each round, participants make strategic choices that impact The Fresh Connection’s supply chain. As the rounds progress, additional decision-making options become available, allowing for strategy refinement while also increasing complexity.

At the start of each round, participants begin with the saved strategic choices from the previous round. Every decision involves trade-offs, requiring careful evaluation of potential benefits versus negative consequences. To make informed choices, participants should review available information, including dashboards, reports and inbox before finalizing decisions.

More details on each decision can be found in the InfoCenter using the search function or by selecting the tooltips within the business simulation interface.

#### Tactics and Strategy

The Fresh Connection is a strategic and tactical game. The objective is the long-term effects of your decisions on the financial performance of the business. This means that you must think about strategy and aligning your business towards long-term goals (as if your decisions will be in effect over many years).

The negative consequences resulting from poor decisions in a previous round will have little impact on the present round as you will be able to assess and correct any decisions that resulted in negative consequences. Organize the business afresh in every round, reviewing and adjusting decisions to align with your chosen strategy.

Good results from previous rounds are of course important with respect to refining these for making further improvements.



#### General

The goal of The Fresh Connection is to achieve the highest possible score measured as Return on Investment (ROI). Throughout the game, multiple performance measures are calculated, including the team measures and individual role measures. The final ranking is determined based on either the highest ROI achieved in the individual rounds or the highest weighted average ROI across all rounds.

#### Team

The team score is based on Return on Investment (ROI). More details on ROI calculation can be found in Important Topics > Finance. Team performance is dependent on application of technical and business knowledge, and how well the team engages in communication and collaborative decision-making.

#### Purchasing

The VP of Purchasing’s score is a weighted average of the contract index based on terms negotiated with suppliers. Lower contract index result in lower component unit price and a higher score for the VP of Purchasing.

#### Operations

The VP of Operations’ score is based on production and warehousing costs. Lower costs relative to revenue lead to a higher score for the VP of Operations.

#### Sales Rooms

The VP of Sales’ score is determined solely by revenue. Higher contracted sales revenue results in a higher score for the VP of Sales.

#### Supply Chain Management

The VP of Supply Chain’s score is based on inventory value. A lower combined component and finished goods inventory value results in a higher score for the VP of Supply Chain.



#### Changes

During a round, decisions can be changed as many times as needed without consequences, as only the final saved decisions before the round is calculated will count.

However, there are exceptions when certain conditions are present depending on the set-up:

- Terminating a purchasing contract that was agreed upon for multiple rounds will result in a contract cancellation cost. The associated contract value and costs for the remaining duration will remain unchanged.
- Decisions related to obtaining additional information using software tools **within the simulation** or ordering reports **from a third party** cannot be reversed, as the requested information is provided instantly.

Once a round has been calculated, the results are final, and no further changes can be made.

#### Save

Each strategic choice must be saved by selecting the appropriate "**Save**" button or by finalizing a "**Deal**" with a supplier or customer.

After clicking the "**Save"** button, a blue bar will appear at the top of the screen, confirming that the results have been saved.

If the blue bar does not appear, the choices have not been saved. To ensure accuracy, it is recommended to reload the page, which will update the system with the most recent data from the database.

#### General

Each team member has four Key Performance Indicators (KPIs). At the start of the simulation, these KPIs are fixed. Participants can view them on the My Company screen, located at the bottom. In a later round, depending on the set-up you can adjust the KPIs with alternate performoance measures.

The team’s KPIs are compared against the pool average, and alerts will notify teams if they underperform on a selected KPI. These alerts are displayed in the text box on the right side of the My Company screen.

Additionally, KPI performance can be reviewed on the results page for each role, where scores are benchmarked against other teams in the pool, excluding the bottom and top 10% of performers. The highest-scoring team’s results are also displayed, allowing for direct performance benchmarking.

#### ROI (%) KING (%)

Return on Investment (ROI) is a measure of financial performance, calculated by dividing the net profit (or benefits) of an investment by its total cost. ROI is expressed as a percentage and is used to evaluate the efficiency and profitability of an investment.

In The Fresh Connection, ROI is measured as Operating Profit as a percentage of Investment over a six-month period.

#### Economic Inventory (weeks)

Represents the total finished product inventory available to fulfill future demand, considering:

1. Current stock levels
2. Planned production replenishment
3. Outstanding backorders

**Calculation:** Economic Inventory = On-hand Inventory + Planned Production – Backorders. Measured in weeks.

#### Economic inventory components (weeks)

Represents the total component inventory available for production, considering:

1. Current stock levels
2. Planned supplier deliveries
3. Stock required for planned production

**Calculation:** Economic Inventory Components = On-hand Component Inventory + Planned Supplier Deliveries – Inventory for Planned Production. Measured in weeks.

#### Supplier selection

The VP of Purchasing selects suppliers from the supplier market. Unless dual sourcing is available, only one supplier per component type can be selected. Each supplier has unique characteristics, including: Locatio, Free capacity, Certification. The default delivery terms can be negotiated with the selected supplier.

#### Cancelling contracts

It is possible to cancel an existing contract with a supplier. However, cancellation incurs a cost. Cancellation costs depend on: Remaining contract duration and contract value (purchasing value). Once accepted, this decision cannot be undone, so consider the consequences carefully.

#### Expiring contracts

Contract durations decrease by one round each new round. If a contract is not extended, it will expire. A notification email will appear in the Inbox when a contract is about to expire. Participants can either: Select a new supplier (free of charge) or offer a contract extension to the current supplier. If the contract expires and no new supplier is selected, components of that type will no longer be supplied.

#### No supplier selected

If a supplier is not selected—whether by accident or intentionally—the corresponding component will not be delivered. As a result, the finished product that depends on this component cannot be produced or sold, negatively impacting sales and revenue. To avoid this, ensure a supplier is selected for every required component.

#### Negotiations

The VP of Purchasing can negotiate delivery terms with suppliers, covering aspects like trade unit size, delivery windows, contract duration, payment terms, and review periods. Higher requirements on a supplier will result in a higher contract index and purchase price, while lower requirements can lead to cost savings.

#### Contract index

A pricing mechanism that reflects the agreed service level with a supplier. Each component has a fixed base price, and the contract index acts as a multiplier to this price. A higher contract index (e.g., 1.05) increases costs by 5%, while a lower index (e.g., 0.95) results in a 5% discount. The index is influenced by the level of service requirements imposed on the supplier.

#### Contract duration

Defines how long a contract remains in effect. Longer contracts enhance delivery reliability but are more expensive to terminate. Suppliers prefer longer-term commitments, as they provide stability. Shorter contracts offer flexibility but may come with less favorable terms. If termination is necessary, costs depend on the remaining contract duration and value.

#### Agreed delivery reliability (%)

The percentage of units that should be delivered on or before the promised time. The supplier's ability to meet this commitment is influenced by free capacity and certification.

#### Lead time (days)

The number of days between order placement and delivery. Lead times vary by supplier and location, with suppliers further away typically having longer lead times.

#### Delivery window

The timeframe within which delivery must occur. A shorter window (e.g., 4 hours) ensures precise delivery but may come at a premium, while a longer window (e.g., 1 week) provides flexibility, allowing suppliers to deliver earlier or later within the agreed timeframe. Suppliers typically prefer longer delivery windows, as they provide greater operational flexibility.

#### Transport costs

Costs associated with moving components from suppliers to The Fresh Connection’s warehouse. Transport costs are influenced by the supplier's location—greater distances result in higher costs. Additionally, transport mode impacts costs; sea shipping is generally cheaper than road transport but may involve longer lead times.

#### Transport mode 

The method used for shipping components. Transport by boat is cost-effective but has longer lead times, while transport by truck is faster but more expensive. Some suppliers offer transport mode flexibility, allowing negotiation based on cost, speed, and operational requirements.

#### Trade unit

Defines the standard unit size for ordering from suppliers and is both the Order Minimum and the Order Multiple. For example, Full Truck Loads (FTLs) arrive as full truck deliveries as a minimum and will always deliver a full truck. Packaging materials are typically shipped on pallets, while liquid components like pulp and additives can be delivered in drums (250-liter content), Intermediate Bulk Containers (IBCs) (1,000-liter content) or tanks (30,000-liter-content). Larger trade units reduce handling and administrative costs, improving contract terms but increase inventory levels and warehouse capacity needs.

#### Quality

Component quality can be categorized as “poor,” “middle,” or “high.” Higher quality components are more expensive but can reduce production disruptions. While the quality of sourced components does not impact the final product’s market value, it does affect production efficiency. Poor-quality packaging materials may cause bottling line breakdowns, affecting Production Plan Adherence. Finished products are tested post-production to ensure they meet The Fresh Connection’s standards for aroma, color, and taste. Any product that fails the test is scrapped. Quality control can be enhanced by implementing raw material inspections during component intake.

#### Free capacity

The portion of a supplier’s total capacity that is not currently utilized. A supplier with high free capacity is more likely to process The Fresh Connection’s orders on time and meet agreed delivery reliability. Conversely, a supplier with low free capacity may struggle to fulfill orders on time due to reaching its operational limits.

#### Certification

Indicates whether a supplier has internal processes in place to control production. Certified suppliers are generally more reliable in both delivery performance and component quality.

#### Payment terms (weeks)

The timeframe within which the supplier must be paid. Suppliers typically prefer shorter payment terms.

#### Payment terms effectuation

Specifies whether payment terms take effect at the time of ordering or at the time of delivery. This is a characteristic of each supplier.

#### VMI

Vendor Managed Inventory (VMI) is a collaboration model where the supplier manages, monitors, and replenishes inventory levels at the customer’s location based on pre-agreed stock levels and expected demand. The supplier is responsible for maintaining stock within an agreed range, overriding the VP of Supply Chain’s decisions on safety stock and lot size. Suppliers often offer discounts under VMI agreements, with the discount amount depending on factors such as stock range flexibility, lead time, and component type.

A VMI project costs €5,000 per year to implement.

Note: VMI cannot be applied to secondary suppliers in a dual-sourcing setup.

#### Supplier development

Supplier development is a collaboration initiative aimed at improving supplier performance and facilitating the certification of their production processes. Implementing a supplier development program enhances reliability, component quality, and reduces CO2 emissions. The program costs €60,000 per year per supplier. The success of this initiative depends on the supplier’s importance to The Fresh Connection (in terms of purchased volume) and the duration of the contract. Suppliers are not obliged to accept this collaboration initiative.

#### Location

The country where the supplier is based.

#### Selecting a dual source

Dual sourcing allows the VP of Purchasing to select a secondary supplier for each component, in addition to the primary supplier. Only one secondary supplier can be selected per component type. To enable dual sourcing, navigate to the supplier market tab and select ""Dual source."" Keep in mind that managing an additional supplier incurs an annual cost of €40,000 per supplier.

#### Function of a dual source

If dual sourcing is enabled, replenishment orders are issued to the secondary supplier if The Fresh Connection risks running out of stock. The order size in such cases is one week of planned demand for the component. Dual sourcing serves as a risk mitigation strategy to reduce supply chain disruptions.

#### Raw materials inspection

#### Bottling line selection

The bottling lines are responsible for filling packaging with mixed juice. There are different types of lines: efficient lines, flexible lines, and all-round lines. An efficient line has a high filling capacity but longer changeover times, while a flexible line has shorter changeovers but a lower filling capacity. #### Bottling line characteristics

Each bottling line has specific characteristics, including capacity, staffing needs, sensitivity to breakdowns, and changeover times. When switching from one product to another, a changeover is required. A formula changeover occurs when the packaging remains the same but the juice mixture changes, while a size changeover takes longer because the packaging type or size also changes.

Once a bottling line starts after a changeover, an initial start-up productivity loss is inevitable due to tuning issues. This may cause defects such as partly filled packs or missing caps. However, this loss typically only occurs in the first hour of production, after which the bottling line operates normally.

Implementing Track & Trace costs €10,000 annually.

#### Negotiations

The VP of Sales negotiates delivery terms with customers, covering aspects such as order quantities, service levels, shelf life, and payment terms. #### Contract index

The contract index determines whether a customer pays a premium or receives a discount on the basic sales price. It is calculated based on the Service Level Agreement (SLA) terms.

- A contract index of 1.05 means the customer agrees to pay 5% above the basic price, reflecting stricter SLA terms.
- A contract index of 0.95 means the customer insists on a 5% discount, likely due to less favorable terms.

The contract index is not the sole determinant of the final price. If service levels are not met, customers apply penalties. Conversely, if service levels exceed expectations, a customer may offer a small bonus. The bonus is generally lower than what could have been achieved through negotiating the achieved added-value to service level contracts upfront.

#### Service level

Service level agreements (SLA) are critical performance measures and measureThe Fresh Connection’s reliability to fulfill customer demand on time and in full (OTIF).

Customers prefer reliable suppliers with higher service levels which delivers a higher contract index and results in higher unit prices . However, failing to meet the promised service level may lead to penalties and an effective higher discounts on the contract index, impacting the final price.

While a higher service level can attract a premium, it also introduces risk if the supply chain fails to meet the agreed standards. If the actual service level exceeds the agreed terms, the customer may offer a small bonus. The bonus is generally lower than what could have been achieved through negotiating the achieved added-value to service level contracts upfront.

The VP of Sales must carefully balance service level commitments with supply chain feasibility to ensure realistic agreements that optimize revenue without increasing risks.

#### Service level type

The service level is a measure of The Fresh Connection's ability to meet customer demand on time and in full (OTIF). It can be calculated in different ways, depending on the agreement with the customer. The service level can be measured as:

- The total number of units delivered on time and in full as a percentage of the total number of ordered units.
- The total number of order lines delivered on time and in full as a percentage of the total order lines.
- The total number of orders fulfilled on time and in full as a percentage of the total requested orders.

It's generally easier to achieve higher performance levels at the lower aggregate.

**Example**: One order may include 5 order lines. The Fresh Connection may deliver On Time and In Full on 4 of those order lines. This will result in an 80% OTIF Service Delivery on an Order Line level (4/5), and 0% OTIF at an Order Level (0/1). While four of the order lines on that order were delivered, at an Order Level measure the order will not be considered On Time In Full.

#### Requested shelf life (%)

The VP of Sales can negotiate the minimum shelf-life requirement in the Service Level Agreement (SLA) as a percentage of total shelf life. Fresh juice is a perishable product with a limited shelf life that starts as soon as the finished products leave the bottling line. A higher shelf-life is a significant added-value and increases the contract index, as customers are willing to pay more for fresher products. Customers prefer to receive products with the longest possible remaining shelf life to allow longer storage and offer consumers a longer shelf-life.

**Example:** If a product has a 20-week shelf life and the SLA requires a 70% shelf life, then only products with at least 14 weeks of remaining shelf life will be delivered.

At the same time, The Fresh Connection must maintain a safety stock to ensure it can fulfill orders. The higher the shelf-life requirement, the lower the stock level in weeks that The Fresh Connection can maintain before the product becomes obsolete.

**Minimum shelf-life requirement:** 40%

**Maximum shelf-life requirement:** 85%

#### Shortage rule

If The Fresh Connection's stock of finished products is insufficient to meet customer demand, the shortage rule determines how remaining stock is allocated:

- **Proportional Method:** The shortage is equally distributed across all customer orders for the day.
- **First-Come-First-Served:** Orders are fulfilled based on order time, with earlier orders receiving priority.
- **Customer Priority Principle:** The VP of Sales ranks customers by priority, with higher-priority customers receiving deliveries first. The lowest-priority customers experience the greatest impact from shortages.

#### Priority

#### Frozen period of production (weeks)

Each week, The Fresh Connection’s planning division issues a new production plan, determining which finished products will be produced and when. A key aspect of the production plan is the frozen period of production, which represents the number of weeks for which the production schedule is fixed and cannot be changed.

For example, if the frozen period of production is set to two weeks, the schedule for the next two weeks is locked. Beyond this period, the production plan can still be adjusted.

A long frozen period offers advantages, particularly for procurement and production planning:

- The component requirements are precisely known, ensuring timely and accurate deliveries.
- Production leveling is improved, leading to a more evenly distributed capacity usage.

However, longer frozen periods also reduce flexibility:

- If demand suddenly increases, production cannot be adjusted quickly to meet it.
- Customer service may be negatively affected if there are unexpected peaks in demand and insufficient stock to fulfill orders.

Balancing production stability with responsiveness is key when determining the appropriate frozen period length.

#### General

Each product and component has a fixed standard price, visible in the Information section. Sales and Purchasing roles cannot negotiate prices directly.

Instead, negotiation occurs through service level agreements (SLAs). A contract index is calculated based on SLA terms: Better SLA terms → Higher contract index → Higher sales price / Lower purchase cost.

The contract index acts as a multiplier to the base price, impacting final costs.

#### Finished product

Each product has a fixed basic sales price, which can be found in the Information section. However, the final sales price depends on the contract index, calculated based on the terms of the service level agreement (SLA) negotiated by the VP of Sales. A higher contract index reflects favorable SLA terms, leading to a higher sales price, while a lower index results in a discount.

For instance, a contract index of 1.05 means the customer pays 5% more than the basic price, whereas 0.95 indicates a 5% discount. However, the final price is determined not only by negotiations but also by the actual realization of the agreed SLA terms. If service commitments are not met, customers may demand penalties or discounts.

#### Raw materials

Like finished products, components also have a fixed basic purchase price visible in the Information section. The final purchase price is determined by the contract index, which depends on the terms negotiated by the VP of Purchasing.

Suppliers offering relaxed delivery terms may provide a lower contract index (e.g., 0.95 = 5% discount), whereas stricter delivery terms lead to a higher contract index (e.g., 1.05 = 5% premium).

While all suppliers offer the same basic price for a component, their starting contract index may differ, affecting the final purchase cost.

#### Customers

The Fresh Connection serves various customers, including retailers and convenience stores, each with their own delivery requirements.

#### Finished product

All finished products have a fixed basic price, but the final sales price depends on customer satisfaction with supply chain performance. Good performance may result in a premium price, while poor performance leads to penalty discounts.

Packaging affects storage efficiency, as items fit into outer boxes, pallets, and layers. Products have a limited shelf life, and customers require delivery within a specified period to ensure they retain the product for a portion of its shelf life. If stock is held too long, it cannot be sold and must be discarded.

Each bottling line has specific production capacities, including:

- Changeover times
- Breakdown sensitivity
- Packaging material tolerances
- Operator requirements
- Depreciation and maintenance costs

Mixing is constrained by technical capacity limits:

- Minimum batch size (to ensure proper mixing)
- Maximum capacity (maximum liters per batch)
- Run time (time required to mix one batch)
- Cleaning time (after each juice flavor is mixed)

Additionally, there are variable costs per mixing hour and fixed costs for depreciation and maintenance.

#### Raw materials

All components have a fixed basic price, but the actual purchase price depends on supplier negotiations. Stricter supply terms → Higher price. More flexible supply terms → Lower price

**Packaging formats**:

- Large rolls → Delivered on pallets
- Pulp & additives → Supplied in drums, IBCs (Intermediate Bulk Containers), or tank trucks
- Drum capacity: 250 liters
- IBC capacity: 1,000 liters
- Tank truck capacity: 30,000 liters

**Shelf Life Considerations**:

- Fruit pulp has a limited shelf life and must be used before expiration.
- Storage beyond its usability in The Fresh Connection's tank yard or raw materials warehouse results in waste and financial loss.
- The expiry countdown starts from the supplier’s order date.

#### Suppliers

Each supplier has unchangeable characteristics, including:

- **Location:** Impacts transport costs (distant suppliers → higher costs).
- **Market share:** Indicates supplier size and stability in the world market.
- **Certification:** Certified suppliers are generally more reliable in delivery and material quality.
- **Packaging quality:** Poor packaging leads to higher rejection rates and bottling line issues.

**Lead time:** The period between placing an order and receiving it.

- Distant suppliers → Longer lead times
- Make-To-Order (MTO) production → Longer production times than Make-To-Stock (MTS).
- In-house pulp production → Longer lead times than traders.

**Delivery reliability:** The % of orders delivered within the agreed lead time. However, this is a promise, and suppliers may not always meet it due to:

- Capacity constraints

- Peak demand periods

- Production inefficiencies

- Free capacity: Determines how much additional production a supplier can handle.

- Gross capacity: Total production capacity before contracting with The Fresh Connection.

- Net capacity: Remaining capacity after fulfilling The Fresh Connection’s contract.

- Low net capacity → Higher risk of late deliveries, especially during peak demand.

  #### General

  The Fresh Connection (TFC) computational model converts team decisions into logistical results and individual and team performance scores. While the exact simulation model is not detailed, key interpretation aspects will be covered.

  #### Simulation

  To reduce statistical uncertainty, each round is simulated for 20 years by running 40 iterations of six-month periods. This ensures that team results remain comparable, even when different decisions are made.

  **Key benefits of this approach:**

  - More accurate logistics data: Numbers like consignments received are averaged over 40 simulations, avoiding misleading whole-number fluctuations.
  - Reliable graphical reports: Charts and figures reflect consistent patterns, reducing discrepancies between visual data and actual calculations.

  By using this method, The Fresh Connection ensures fair comparisons and realistic insights across different teams.

  #### Customer demand

  Customer demand is a critical factor in the simulation model. While base demand parameters are identical for all teams to ensure comparability, each team can influence demand patterns through negotiations with customers.

  **Key influences on demand:**

  - Sales Volume Rebates: Offering rebates can make demand more volatile and impact purchase behavior.
  - Promotional Pressure: Increased promotional efforts typically boost demand.

  To support stock replenishment, The Fresh Connection uses a weekly forecast model based on demand patterns. While teams cannot adjust the baseline forecast, they can enhance accuracy by negotiating agreements with customers and improving promotional forecasts.

  #### Delivery

  If The Fresh Connection cannot deliver an order line in full, it remains on backorder and will be delivered once stock is available. However, delayed fulfillment lowers the realized service level, which can result in penalties or revenue loss.

  #### Shelf life

  The model tracks when each finished product is produced. Shelf life starts immediately, and products nearing expiry are prioritized for delivery. Stock that exceeds shelf life and cannot be sold is written off as obsolete.

  #### Production planning

  Each team generates a weekly production plan, aiming to spread production orders evenly to minimize capacity peaks. However, bottlenecks like limited production capacity and component shortages may disrupt plan adherence.

  #### Component demand

  Component demand is based on the Bill of Materials and production planning. The system forecasts demand for each component, and if stock runs low, it automatically triggers a replenishment order from the chosen supplier.

  #### Supply

  #### Forecast error

  The Mean Absolute Percentage Error is a measure of the forecast unreliability.

  It is determined by specifying the absolute forecast error (demand minus forecast) on a weekly basis. The sum of the forecast errors is then divided by the sum of the demand.

  A MAPE of 0% is the ideal, in which case the demand is equal to the forecast. If the MAPE is high then the forecast is unreliable.

  #### Stock development

  #### Bias (%)

  Forecast bias measures the systematic difference between predicted and actual demand.

  It is calculated as **(forecast - demand) ÷ demand**.

  - Positive bias indicates that the forecast was higher than actual demand, leading to excess inventory, increased holding costs, and a higher risk of obsolescence.
  - Negative bias means the forecast was lower than demand, which can result in low stock levels and potential shortages of components for production.

  Since both positive and negative biases can create operational challenges, maintaining forecast accuracy is essential for optimizing inventory and production efficiency.

  #### Warehousing report

  This report tracks the storage and movement of components and finished products across three key areas: the raw materials warehouse, the tank yard, and the finished goods warehouse.

  #### Capacity

  Represents the storage capacity of a warehouse or tank yard, measured by the number of pallet locations or tanks.

  #### Usage

  Tracks the average number of pallets or tanks in use. If usage exceeds capacity, overflow occurs:

  - Tank shortages force pulp storage in IBCs within the inbound warehouse.
  - Inbound warehouse shortages lead to overflow into another warehouse.

  #### Cube utilization (%)

  Measures warehouse or tank yard storage efficiency, calculated as:

  **(Pallets or tanks in stock ÷ total pallet locations or tanks) × 100**

  A utilization rate above 100% signals structural undercapacity. Even at less than 100% utilization, overflow may still occur due to peak demand fluctuations.

  #### Overflow (%)

  **Warehouses:** Tracks overflow warehouse usage as a percentage of total pallet location days.

  **Tank yard:** Measures the volume pumped into IBCs due to tank yard space constraints, expressed as a percentage of total supplied volume.

  Overflow may occur even when utilization is below 100%, as a buffer for demand surges.
